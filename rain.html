RAIN

  • Year: 2012–2013
  • Medium: 3 Parts: Interactive media box / Projection / Prints ; Box: 356mm x 710mm x 60mm, cherrywood ; 5 custom-made sliderknobs, cherrywood ; 2 Buttons (refresh and pause) ; Screen: 266mm x 474mm
  • Exhibitions: media.art.collecting – Perspectives of a collection, curated by: Günther Holler-Schuster, Katrin Bucher Trantow, Katia Huemer Kunsthaus Graz, Austria
    2012/06/16 – 2013/06/02

Rain by LIA in collaboration with engineer Damian Stewart, is “an abstract artistic interpretation of raindrops falling on a window pane” and explores the natural phenomenon “of patterns emerging from the random forms created when rain falls onto a window.” The software-based system generates continuously changing visual output: one version is an interactive box (with five custom-made slider knobs and two buttons) where viewers can tweak parameters of the generative algorithm; another version is a projection of the software autonomously moving through parameter states; a third is a series of digital prints capturing selected “moments” of the algorithmic output.

The media box was made from cherrywood and aluminium, and it embeds a low-powered computer running Linux that requires little to no maintenance. This hardware design raises questions about how software-based art should present itself—aesthetic, convenient, user-friendly. Stewart comments that collectors don’t want to buy “a computer” and face upgrade/maintenance issues; they would rather collect “objects that ‘just work’.”

This piece invites viewers into an active role as parameter-changers rather than passive observers, shifting the dynamic of art consumption: you aren’t just watching rain patterns, but directly manipulating them via the sliders, which underscores a broader trend in interactive and new-media art. The hardware design also signals a maturation of software art towards collectors and potential users as well-designed objects of interest rather than utilitarian screen-based installations. The fact that the work is available in editions, prints, and physical boxes highlights how generative art is being folded into the commercial/collecting art world.
