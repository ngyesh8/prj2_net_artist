ABOUT

LIA is an Austrian pseudonymous artist and pioneer who has been exploring software art since 1995. She uses generative audiovisual compositions (via code) to create organic, fin-de-siècle Viennese abstraction, presenting her work through a range of digital applications, many of which are interactive. Her goal is to establish harmony between artistic intent, viewer input, and the seeming randomness created by algorithms. Suggesting that computers are not just tools, but an interplay of complex forces that collectively form legible and nuanced compositions.


Currently Based in Vienna,
Duration of work (1995 – Present)

HISTORICAL RELEVANCE

A generation of Austrian artists from the mid-1990s onward, including LIA, turned to digital, video, web-based, and software media to revive abstraction in new, generative, and systems-oriented modes. Early computing and algorithmic-art practices (from the 1950s to the 1980s) treated code, machinery, and technology not merely as tools but as aesthetic media in their own right—turning industrial/military-intended systems into frameworks for form, abstraction, and visual experimentation. Since many of the works are prints or photographs of electronic processes, seeing the actual physical work (rather than just a reproduction) is important for understanding their texture, process, and aesthetic impact as their audiences begin to enter the changing landscape of the digital world. These works maintain a “critical distance” from mainstream digital imagery (like commercial computer graphics or mass-media visuals). They are not simply design or advertising, but artifacts of a parallel art-technology culture. From web work to installations, LIA became known as a pioneer in the Austrian Abstracts movement—bridging the legacy of early computer art with contemporary digital aesthetics.
