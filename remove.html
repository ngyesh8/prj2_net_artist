RE-MOVE

  • Year: 1999-2003; Rebuilt 2023-2025
  • Medium: Interactive Generative Art (p5js, formerly Director)
  • Original Format: Web & Limited Edition Artist CD-ROM
  • Exhibitions & Awards: Award of Distinction, Prix Ars Electronica (2003), MAXXI, National Museum of 21st Century Arts, Rome (2007), Biennale de Montréal (CIAC) (2002), LABoral Centro de Arte y Creación Industrial, Gijón (2007), Recognized in Rhizome’s Net Art Anthology

Following her collaboration with the artist Dextro on the influential 1997 project Turux.org, LIA developed her solo series re-move as a continuation and deepening of her exploration. While Turux.org had introduced her to creating interactive audiovisual compositions using Macromedia Director, re-move became a more personal and conceptually refined project. Macromedia Director was a popular tool among web designers at the time. Marketed with the slogan “Add life to the web!” LIA’s work indeed did so, but through a distinctly artistic lens—her compositions balanced soft, organic forms with geometric precision and a minimal color palette, achieving an aesthetic both restrained and alive. Her work became part of the forefront of what would later be known as generative art.

RE-move, emphasizes discovery and experimentation through interaction. Each of the ten pieces in the series features sets of enigmatic, unlabeled icons that allow users to alter various parameters of the artwork. Without any instructions or explanations, participants are encouraged to explore through trial and error, learning how their actions affect the composition. This process mirrors LIA’s own intuitive approach to coding and design—an open-ended dialogue between human intention, algorithmic logic, and chance. The user can never fully control the system; rather, they collaborate with it, participating in a shared process of creation.

LIA also extended the generative philosophy into the physical design of the work’s limited-edition CD-ROM release. Each CD was enclosed in a hand-sewn plastic cover filled with fragrant orange blossoms and a floating transparent film bearing the project’s title. The shifting position of these elements ensured that every package was unique, reflecting the unpredictability and individuality of the digital works themselves. The CD-ROM’s 800×600 resolution, typical of late-1990s multimedia design, was chosen deliberately to preserve the intimate scale and clarity of the visuals. Over time, some pieces in re-move became technically fragile. Certain compositions depended on specific software behaviors—such as glitches within Director—that could not be exactly reproduced in newer environments. Recognizing this, LIA undertook a careful digital preservation effort, rebuilding the entire series in p5.js while maintaining its original resolution and feel. This act not only preserved accessibility but also highlighted the time-specific nature of early digital art, where code, aesthetics, and technology were inseparable.

Re-move represents a pivotal moment in LIA’s career and in the history of digital art. It bridges early web-based experimentation with a mature investigation of interaction, system-based creativity, and the aesthetics of code—showcasing not what machines are supposed to be, but what they could be.

“This is the larger ideological importance of software-driven abstraction–at its best it quite accurately and at the same time poetically captures our new image of a world seen as the dynamic networks of relations, oscillating between order and disorder, always vulnerable and ready to change with a single click of the user.”

– Lev Manovich
